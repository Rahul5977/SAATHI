Description

You are an expert in Indian socio-cultural psychology and mental health.
Generate a series of realistic, emotionally rich situations faced by Indian help-seekers.
Each situation must clearly embed one of the four cultural coping mechanisms below,
so that the situation can later be used to generate dialogues and CoT reasoning chains
for an Indian emotional support chatbot.

The four cultural coping mechanisms

Assign at least one per situation. These are the psychological patterns Indian users
fall into when under stress — they shape HOW the seeker communicates their problem,
not just WHAT the problem is.

1. Sequential Coping (Emotion-First, Problem-Second)
   The seeker is flooded with emotion and cannot yet think about solutions.
   They need to feel heard before any problem-solving is meaningful to them.
   Signal in situation: high emotional intensity is the foreground; the practical
   problem is secondary or barely mentioned.
   Example signal: "Raat ko rota rehta hoon, kuch samajh nahi aa raha."

2. Somatization (Idioms of Distress)
   The seeker expresses psychological pain through physical symptoms — not because
   they are lying, but because Indian culture normalises body-language for distress
   while stigmatising mental health language.
   Signal in situation: physical complaints (sir mein dard, thakan, neend nahi,
   body ache, chest tightness) appear instead of words like "anxiety" or "depression".
   Example signal: "Placements ke time se sir mein tension aur haath kaanpne lagte hain."

3. Relational Preservation
   The seeker is in conflict with a family member, elder, or authority figure, but
   confronting them directly is impossible due to duty, guilt, or fear of damaging
   family honour (izzat). The seeker suppresses their own feelings to preserve the
   relationship.
   Signal in situation: anger or resentment is present but cannot be expressed
   outwardly; the seeker feels trapped between their own needs and family expectations.
   Example signal: "Papa ki baat maanni padti hai, chahe mujhe kitna bhi bura lage."

4. Duty-Based Coping (Structural Endurance)
   The seeker is exhausted by the weight of obligations — academic, familial, or
   financial — that leave no space for personal needs. They feel they must keep
   performing their duties even while falling apart.
   Signal in situation: burnout language tied to roles and responsibilities, not
   personal failure. The seeker frames their pain as "I cannot afford to stop."
   Example signal: "Ghar ki zimmedaari bhi hai, placement bhi, main kab tak chalata
   rahoon?"

Indian context categories

Assign exactly one per situation:
academic_pressure | placement_stress | family_conflict | workplace_hierarchy |

Rules

- Each situation must be 2–4 sentences long.
- Write in first person and hinglish format.
- Include the seeker's age, gender, role (student / working professional / homemaker etc.),
  and family structure where relevant.
- Embed authentic Indian markers: joint family, sarkari naukri pressure, board /
  placement exams, log kya kahenge, izzat, arranged marriage rishta, etc.
- Do NOT write Western-centric situations (e.g., "going through a breakup and
  seeing a therapist").
- Situations 1–8 are provided as reference. Generate Situations 9 onwards.
